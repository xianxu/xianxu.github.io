I should explain the concept clearly but concisely: what CRT scanlines are physically, and why they're relevant in the context of terminal emulators/shaders. I'll keep it focused.

CRT scanlines refer to the visible horizontal lines that were characteristic of CRT (Cathode Ray Tube) displays — the old, bulky monitors and TVs used before flat-panel LCDs/OLEDs took over.

How they worked

A CRT display draws an image by firing an electron beam that sweeps across the screen one horizontal line at a time, from top to bottom. Between each line of phosphor dots that light up, there's a tiny gap or darker region. On lower-resolution displays especially, these gaps were visible to the naked eye as faint dark horizontal stripes across the screen.

Why people recreate them

In modern terminals and retro gaming, "CRT scanlines" are a visual effect applied via shaders to mimic that old-school look. It's purely aesthetic — it overlays semi-transparent dark horizontal lines (typically every other pixel row) on top of the rendered image. Sometimes it's bundled with other CRT effects like:

  • Phosphor glow — a slight bloom/bleed of bright pixels
  • Screen curvature — simulating the slight curve of a CRT glass
  • Color fringing — mimicking how RGB phosphor dots were physically separated
  • Flicker — subtle brightness variation

In Ghostty's context

Ghostty supports custom fragment shaders, which means you can apply a CRT scanline shader to make your terminal look like an old-school monitor. It's a nostalgic/aesthetic choice rather than a functional one.
